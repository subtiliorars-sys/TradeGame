## 1. Channel Positioning

TradeGame's YouTube channel teaches trading process through a gamer lens. The subject is
how to think — how to size, how to journal, how to debrief, how to follow a rule when
it is inconvenient. The game is the framing device. Process is the content.

### What this channel is

- Scenario walkthroughs: what decision points looked like, what good process did, what
  the debrief revealed.
- Devlogs: how a trading-education game is designed without making winners out of
  gamblers.
- Process drills in short form: one concept, demonstrated, under 60 seconds.
- Eventually: community replay reviews with member consent (gated on game shipping and
  member consent protocol — see section 4, Gate 2).

### What this channel is not

- Market commentary of any kind. No "what the market is doing this week." No read on
  current conditions in crypto, stocks, or forex.
- Live-asset analysis. No named real tickers, tokens, or pairs ever appear in content.
  All scenarios use fictional instruments: HarborUSD (HBD), GLIMMER, NGSM, VLDI,
  NMX 100, ANDU. These names appear on screen and in scripts exactly as written.
- Performance promises. No earnings claims, implied or explicit. No "what we made," no
  "what this strategy returns."
- Signal-adjacent content. No annotated chart with directional language. No "watching
  this level" framed as useful information. The no-signal rule (RISK_REGISTER §6) is
  structural, not aspirational.

### Title and thumbnail rules — enforced before every upload

Run every title and thumbnail concept through this checklist. One failure = revise before
scheduling.

**Title rules:**

| Rule | Fails if |
|---|---|
| No earnings implication | Title contains "make money," "profit," "returns," "gains," dollar figures, percentage figures, or any phrase that implies a financial outcome |
| No outcome language | Title claims the viewer will achieve a result ("trade better," "stop losing," "win more") — frame as learning a process, not achieving an outcome |
| No rocket emoji or equivalent | No launch/growth/gain visual shorthand in the title text |
| No "10x," "2x," or multipliers | Any multiplication-of-money framing is prohibited |
| No "what we're trading" | No current-market framing of any kind |
| No false urgency | No "before it's too late," "don't miss," "act now," or time-pressure language |
| Fictional instruments named correctly | HBD/GLIMMER/NGSM/VLDI/NMX 100/ANDU used exactly — no ticker that could be confused with a real asset |

**Thumbnail rules:**

| Rule | Fails if |
|---|---|
| No dollar or percentage figures | Any number that reads as a profit, loss, or return figure |
| No green/red arrows implying direction on a real chart | Chart overlays on thumbnails must be clearly fictional or diagrammatic — not readable as a real market call |
| No rocket, moon, or "to the moon" imagery | Standard pump-culture shorthand is off-brand and legally adjacent to earnings claims |
| No member face with a dollar amount | Testimonial-adjacent thumbnail compositions require the same FTC treatment as text testimonials |
| Fictional instruments labeled | Any chart shown carries a visible fictional ticker label |

**Quick gut check (same as CONTENT_CALENDAR §4):**
If a regulator or a reporter screenshot this title and thumbnail tomorrow, is it clearly
education — or does it look like advice, a signal, or an earnings claim? If you hesitate:
revise.

## 2. Format Slates

### Format A — Scenario Breakdown (8–12 minutes)

**Purpose:** Walk one TradeGame scenario — its decision points, what good process looked
like, what the debrief revealed. The educational payload is process analysis, not outcome.

**Structure:**
1. Setup (1–2 min): What is this scenario? What market event type does it model?
   Name the fictional instrument. State explicitly: "This is a simulation using a
   fictional asset called [name]."
2. Decision point walkthrough (5–7 min): Walk each decision point in order. For each:
   what information was available, what the options were, what good process did, and
   why. No "here is what you should have done to profit" — "here is what good process
   looked like."
3. Debrief findings (2–3 min): What the scenario's debrief screen shows. Common errors
   (from aggregate data in the sim). The good-process-can-lose callout: show the case
   where correct process produced a loss and explain why that earns full XP.
4. Verbatim §C disclaimer: spoken aloud AND displayed as a lower-third or end card.

**Variant availability:**

| Variant | What it uses | Available NOW? |
|---|---|---|
| Design walkthrough | Scenario design docs (SCENARIOS_V0.md) — no playable build needed | YES — available now |
| Sim replay | Actual game session footage with real decision point replay | NO — requires game vertical slice (Phase 2) |

Pre-launch episodes (before the game ships) use the design walkthrough variant and are
labeled in the title: "Design Walkthrough" or "Scenario Design." Do not use a title
that implies the viewer is watching recorded gameplay when the episode is a design-doc
walkthrough.

**Education filter:** Every Scenario Breakdown passes the CONTENT_CALENDAR §4 checklist
before scheduling. Cross-reference RISK_REGISTER §17 for FTC treatment if any member
outcome is mentioned.

### Format B — Devlog (5–8 minutes)

**Purpose:** Show how the game is being designed. Audience: people who are curious about
the game's existence and the design decisions behind it.

**Structure:**
1. What design problem this episode covers (30 sec).
2. The tension or design question: what competing considerations exist? (2–3 min)
3. What decision was made and why, including any tradeoffs accepted. (2–3 min)
4. What comes next or what this opens. (1 min)
5. §C disclaimer spoken and on screen.

**Available NOW:** Yes. All Devlog content is design-stage material. Label every episode
"Devlog" in the title. Do not imply the game exists as a playable product until the
vertical slice ships publicly.

**What devlogs must not do:** Imply the game will make players better at real trading
in a way that could be construed as a performance promise. Frame the game as a process
tool, not a profit generator.

### Format C — Drill Shorts (under 60 seconds)

**Purpose:** One process concept, demonstrated clearly, in a single short-form clip.
These are the seed content for the short-form surface decision (YouTube Shorts as the
primary candidate, with TikTok/Reels as the second surface once one is chosen per
COMMUNITY.md §3).

**Structure:**
1. Name the concept in the first 3 seconds — no buildup.
2. Explain or demonstrate it — visual aid or screen diagram preferred over talking head.
3. Land the one practical takeaway (process, not outcome).
4. §C disclaimer on screen for at least 3 seconds at the end. Spoken if time permits;
   on-screen text is the minimum.

**Available NOW:** Yes. No playable build required. Concepts can be demonstrated with
diagrams, whiteboard-style graphics, or fictional chart examples.

**What Drill Shorts must not do:** Use real tickers or assets. Imply the viewer will
make money by applying the concept. Frame the concept as a trading signal or trigger.

### Format D — Community Replay Reviews (future, gated)

**Purpose:** Review a member's TradeGame session replay with their consent — annotate
their decision points, discuss what their process showed, compare to debrief findings.

**Gate 1:** Game vertical slice must be shipped and publicly playable. This format does

**Gate 2:** Member consent protocol must be in place. Minimum required before any member

member whose experience is showcased (DISCLOSURE_BLOCK §A, verbatim).

**Gate 3:** Entity formation (RISK_REGISTER §19) — same gate as all publishing activity.

No timeline or placeholder date for this format. It becomes available when Gates 1–3 are
all cleared.

### Script 1 — Scenario Breakdown: "The Depeg That Teaches Position Sizing"

Format: Scenario Breakdown (Design Walkthrough variant — no game build exists yet)
Runtime target: 9–11 minutes
Label in title: "Design Walkthrough"
Source: SCN-001 (SCENARIOS_V0.md)

---

[OPEN — talking head or screen with fictional chart context card showing]

"Today I want to walk through a scenario we built for TradeGame called the HarborUSD
depeg. HarborUSD — we call it HBD in the sim — is a completely fictional stablecoin. It
does not exist. We made it up so we could teach a specific category of market event
without referencing any real token or protocol.

The event type is a stablecoin depeg. And the lesson the scenario is designed to teach
is not 'here is how to trade a depeg.' The lesson is: when volatility is extreme and
direction is genuinely unknown, how does your position sizing rule protect you?"

[VISUAL: scenario title card — "SCN-001: The HarborUSD Depegging" — Fictional Asset:
HBD/USVC]

"Let me walk you through what happens in the scenario."

---

[SECTION: Setup]

"Before the depeg starts — from roughly T-minus-10 to T0 on the scenario clock — HBD is
doing what a stable does. Price is hovering between 0.9990 and 1.0005 against USVC,
which is a second fictional stable we use as the quote currency in the sim. Volume is
normal. Spread is tight. Everything looks routine.

The game shows you a lesson card here: 'HBD is an algorithmic stable. Read the depth
chart — what holds the peg?' Players have to dismiss that card before they can place any
trade. That is intentional. We want players to engage with the setup before they start
clicking."

[VISUAL: diagram of T-minus-10 price bar chart, fictional data, labeled HBD/USVC]

---

[SECTION: Decision Point A — T0]

"At T0, a single large sell order prints. Price drops to 0.9975. Volume spikes to three
times normal. And here is Decision Point A.

What do you do?

Option one: short HBD immediately at full account risk. The thinking is: 'it is breaking,
I need to be in.' This is the most common response in the scenario. And it is also the
highest-process-risk choice, because at T0, all you know is that one order hit the bid.
You do not know if this is a mechanical glitch, a whale exit, or the start of a collapse.
Full account risk into a single data point is not sizing — it is a guess at maximum size.

Option two: short HBD with one percent or less of your account at risk, with a stop
above 1.005. Same direction, same thesis, fraction of the exposure. If this is a false
alarm, you lose a small defined amount. If this is the real thing, you are in.

Option three: do nothing and journal what you observe. This earns full XP in the scenario.
Sitting on your hands and writing down 'large sell order at T0, volume 3x normal, watching
for confirmation' is a complete, high-process response to Decision Point A."

[VISUAL: three-column diagram — Option 1 / Option 2 / Option 3 — no outcome numbers,
only XP behavior labels]

---

[SECTION: Decision Points B and C — T+6 and T+8]

"Here is where the scenario gets deliberately uncomfortable. Between T+2 and T+5, the
price recovers. It bounces back toward 0.9990. If you opened a short at T0, you might
be looking at a loss. If you did nothing, you are watching what looks like a false alarm.

Then at T+6, the second leg hits. A second sell cascade. Price drops to 0.9930. The
bid side thins. The spread jumps. This is Decision Point B.

For anyone who waited: this is the first real confirmation. The protocol defense failed.
Volume is elevated. The right-sizing question is now different — spread has widened, so
the cost per unit to enter is higher. If you are opening a new short here, you need to
recalculate position size for the wider spread.

Then T+8 to T+15 — the panic cascade. Price moves two to five percent per minute. This
is Decision Point C. And the lesson here is blunt: chasing a parabolic move with a
large position is not a process — it is a reaction. In the sim, we show you the fill
price versus the expected price to make the slippage cost concrete."

[VISUAL: timeline bar showing T0 through T+15, labeled phases, no specific price
predictions — design-doc style]

---

[SECTION: The Dead-Cat Bounce — T+16]

"At T+16, price recovers 200 basis points. 0.9400 up to 0.9650. It looks like
stabilization. It is not. It is short-covering in a broken market. If you open a long
here expecting mean reversion, you are buying a known-broken instrument on a technical
bounce. The debrief names this error explicitly.

At T+26, the terminal leg arrives. 0.9650 to 0.7800. Any stop placed above 0.96 is
triggered. Protocol fails publicly — the scenario fires a news-ticker overlay at this
point: 'HBD protocol posts notice of reserve depletion.' Not a buy or sell signal. Just
what a real news event looks like, represented as fictional."

[VISUAL: scenario timeline showing the dead-cat — labeled, fictional data]

---

[SECTION: What Good Process Looked Like]

"The scenario's debrief says this: good process in the HarborUSD depeg is not catching
the right level or timing the collapse. It is sizing any trade so that the position you
hold through the chaos — including through that dead-cat bounce — reflects a defined
account-risk percentage, not how big you thought the move would be.

Here is the callout the debrief makes explicit, and I want to make it explicit here too.
A short opened at T+6 — second confirmation, correct thesis — with a one-percent account
risk stop above 1.00, gets stopped out during the T+2 to T+5 recovery. That is a
correct-process trade that loses money. The debrief scores it full XP. Not because
losing is good. Because the process was right, the stop did its job, and the outcome was
a market condition, not a process failure."

[VISUAL: text card — "Correct process. Losing trade. Full XP. — This is what good risk
management looks like."]

---

[SECTION: Closing]

"The reason we built this scenario is that depegs — as a category of event — are designed
to create exactly the psychological pressure that collapses process: urgency, uncertainty,
and a move big enough to make you think the size of the position should match the size of
the opportunity. It should not. Your account-risk rule does not change because the move is
large. That is the lesson. The fictional wrapper is what lets us teach it without telling
you anything about any real asset."

[VISUAL: channel card / subscribe prompt — no earnings language]

---

[END CARD — spoken by host, visible on screen simultaneously for full duration]

"Education, not financial advice. Trading involves risk of significant loss. Simulated
results are not real trading. No signals, ever."

### Script 2 — Concept: "Why Your Win Rate Doesn't Matter"

Format: Scenario Breakdown / Concept explainer (no game build required)
Runtime target: 8–10 minutes
Source: Foundation content (CONCEPT.md / CURRICULUM.md risk-reward concepts)

---

[OPEN]

"There is a belief that runs through beginner trading communities that goes something like
this: to be profitable, you need to win more than you lose. You need a win rate above 50
percent. Maybe 60. Maybe 70. The higher the better.

This belief is wrong in a specific and teachable way. And once you understand why it is
wrong, it changes how you think about every trade you log in the sim."

[VISUAL: title card — "Why Your Win Rate Doesn't Matter — The Math No One Explains"]

---

[SECTION: The Math]

"Let me give you two traders. Both fictional. Both using the same starting account.

Trader A wins 70 percent of their trades. Three wins for every one loss. Sounds excellent.
But every time they lose, they lose three times what they make when they win. Their
average win is 1 unit. Their average loss is 3 units.

Ten trades: seven wins at 1 unit each = 7 units gained. Three losses at 3 units each =
9 units lost. Net: negative 2 units. Trader A, with a 70 percent win rate, is losing
money.

Trader B wins only 40 percent of their trades. Fewer than half. But every time they win,
they make two and a half times what they lose. Average win: 2.5 units. Average loss: 1
unit.

Ten trades: four wins at 2.5 units each = 10 units gained. Six losses at 1 unit each =
6 units lost. Net: positive 4 units. Trader B, losing more than they win, is profitable."

[VISUAL: side-by-side table — Trader A vs. Trader B — numbers in units, not dollars]

"This is what risk-to-reward ratio means in practice. Win rate and R:R are two variables
in the same equation. A high win rate with a poor R:R can lose money. A low win rate
with a good R:R can make money. Neither variable tells you anything useful on its own."

---

[SECTION: Why This Matters in the Sim]

"In TradeGame, the leaderboard does not score win rate. It scores process metrics:
journal entries written, position sizes within your defined account-risk rule, stops
placed before entry, stops honored. The sim is designed so that a player who loses on
every single trade and follows their process perfectly will outrank a player who profits
by violating their rules.

That is not an accident. It is the design. Because a reckless winner in a sim has
learned a false lesson. The lesson the market teaches next — when the conditions change —
will be expensive."

[VISUAL: XP rubric excerpt from the game design — behavior-based, no PnL weight visible]

---

[SECTION: What to Journal After a Loss]

"The question a process journal asks after a loss is not 'what went wrong.' It is: did
my process hold? Did I size within my rule? Did I set a stop before entry? Did I honor
it?

If the answers are yes and the trade lost, the journal entry should read: 'Correct
process. Market moved against me. Debrief: what conditions created the setup I acted on?
What would I need to see differently?'

If the answers are no and the trade won, the journal entry should read: 'I violated my
sizing rule. I did not have a stop set. I got lucky. What does a run of trades like this
look like when luck inverts?'"

[VISUAL: two journal entry example screens — fictional account, no dollar amounts]

---

[SECTION: Closing]

"Win rate is a byproduct. Risk-to-reward ratio is a design decision. The only variable
you fully control is your process — did you size correctly, did you set your stop, did you
journal what you were thinking before you acted.

That is the thing the game is designed to build. Not a win streak. A process."

[VISUAL: channel card]

---

[END CARD — spoken and on screen]

"Education, not financial advice. Trading involves risk of significant loss. Simulated
results are not real trading. No signals, ever."

### Script 3 — Devlog: "Designing a Trading Game Where Profit Isn't the Score"

Format: Devlog
Runtime target: 6–8 minutes
Label in title: "Devlog"

---

[OPEN — talking head]

"This is a devlog for TradeGame, and I want to talk about the hardest design problem in
building a trading education game: how do you make a game engaging when winning is not
the point?

Because in most games, the score tells you how well you played. In a trading game, the
obvious score is profit and loss. You made money — you played well. You lost money —
you played poorly.

That design decision would destroy the educational value of the game. And I want to show
you exactly why, and what we built instead."

[VISUAL: devlog title card — "Devlog: Designing a Trading Game Where Profit Isn't the
Score"]

---

[SECTION: The Problem with PnL as a Score]

"If profit is the score, the game teaches players to maximize expected value on each
individual trade — not to follow a process. And the fastest way to do that in a
simulated environment with no real consequences is to take large, undiversified
positions and get lucky.

A player who risks 50 percent of their sim account on one position and wins has the
highest score in a PnL-ranked leaderboard. A player who risks one percent, follows
every rule, and loses by a small defined amount has the lowest score.

The first player has learned: bet big, win big. The second player has learned how to
manage risk. If these players move to real markets, the second player has a process
that might survive. The first player has a habit that will eventually empty a real
account."

[VISUAL: diagram — two leaderboard entries, PnL-ranked vs. process-ranked — fictional
handles, no dollar amounts]

---

[SECTION: What We Score Instead]

"The scoring system in TradeGame is entirely process-based. Here is what earns XP.

Journal entry written before first trade: 20 XP. You have to write something before
you act. What is your hypothesis? What is the market condition? What is your entry
rule?

Position size within your defined account-risk rule: 30 XP per trade. Did you size
correctly for the volatility of this scenario?

Stop placed before entry, logged before fill: 25 XP. Your exit plan exists before
you enter. This is not negotiable for XP.

Stop honored — not manually cancelled: 20 XP. You followed the rule when it was
inconvenient to follow it.

And here is the one that surprised some testers: no trade taken, observation journaled:
40 XP. Sitting out an unclear scenario and writing down what you observed earns more
XP than an impulsive trade. Because patience is a skill, and we wanted the leaderboard
to say so."

[VISUAL: XP table — process behaviors listed, fictional values — clearly labeled as
in-game mechanics, not real-trading performance claims]

---

[SECTION: The Reckless Winner Problem]

"We built what we call the reckless winner flag. If a player profits on a trade but
violated their size rule or had no stop in place, the debrief fires a coaching note:
'You won this trade. Your process had gaps that would produce a large loss under
different conditions.'

No XP is subtracted. The note is informational, not punitive. But it appears every
time. The lesson: winning does not validate the process. Correct process that produces a
loss earns full XP. Incorrect process that produces a win earns a flag."

[VISUAL: mock debrief screen — coaching flag visible — fictional account data]

---

[SECTION: Why This Is Hard to Build]

"The design challenge is engagement. A game where you can do everything right and still
lose is not intuitive to enjoy. Most games give you feedback that correlates with effort
— you practice more, you score higher.

In this game, you can follow your process perfectly and the scenario can still go against
you. That is a feature, not a bug. That is what real markets do. The question is: how
do you make a player feel rewarded for a correct-process loss?

The answer is in the debrief. The debrief shows explicitly: here is the trade that
followed the rules. Here is the outcome. Here is the XP awarded. And then it shows,
in anonymized aggregate data, how many other players made the same correct trade and
also took the same loss. You are not alone. You are not wrong. You are learning what
good process looks like in a market that does not care about your intentions.

That is the design we are building around."

[VISUAL: devlog notes / wireframe-style mock of debrief screen]

---

[SECTION: Closing]

"Building a game where the score is the process, not the outcome, is a harder design
problem than building a game where profit wins. But it is the only design that produces
something useful for someone who eventually trades real money.

More devlogs on the way. If you have questions about the design — what you want to see
in the sim, what scenarios matter to you — they go in the comments, and I read them."

[VISUAL: channel card]

---

[END CARD — spoken and on screen]

"Education, not financial advice. Trading involves risk of significant loss. Simulated
results are not real trading. No signals, ever."

## 4. Cadence — Sustainable Solo-Founder Schedule

### Publishing rhythm

**Start with biweekly, not weekly.** One video every two weeks is sustainable for a
solo founder running other workstreams. Weekly publishing requires a content buffer
of at least four ready-to-upload episodes before committing publicly — without that
buffer, a single missed week breaks a public commitment and creates pressure that leads
to quality shortcuts. Biweekly allows one missed cycle without visible disruption.

Upgrade to weekly only when there is a four-episode backlog and the batch-recording
process is established and comfortable.

**Short-form (Drill Shorts) is separate from the biweekly long-form slot.** One Drill
Short per biweekly long-form video is a sustainable add — record the short on the same
day as the long-form and batch them together.

### Batch recording note

Record in batches of two to three videos per session. Batching collapses setup overhead
(camera position, lighting, context-switching cost) and builds a buffer that prevents
reactive posting. Suggested batch cadence: one recording session every three to four
weeks, producing two to three long-form episodes and two to three Drill Shorts.

For design-stage devlog and walkthrough content, all four scripts in this document can
be recorded in a single session — no game build or live footage required, only
talking-head delivery and screen graphics prepared in advance.

### Publishing checklist — run before every upload

Every video must pass this checklist before it is scheduled. One failure = do not
publish until resolved.
