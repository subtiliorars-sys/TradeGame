Market Monday: What is a market structure break — and why does it matter to your process?

Every chart is a record of decisions made by everyone who traded that asset. A market structure break is a moment when the pattern of higher highs / higher lows (or lower highs / lower lows) changes.

Why does it matter to your *process* — not as a trade signal, but as a concept?

Because knowing what a structure break looks like helps you understand the replay you are reviewing in the sim. When you debrief a scenario, you can ask: was I trading in the direction of structure, or against it? Did I know which?

This is process literacy, not a call. What was the clearest market structure break you noticed in a recent sim run? Drop it in the thread.

Replay Friday: The "I knew it was going to happen" debrief trap.

After a scenario ends, our brain rewrites the story. The move that happened looks obvious in hindsight. This is called hindsight bias, and it is one of the most dangerous things that can happen during a replay session.

A better debrief question: what did you know *before* the move? What was your process telling you at each decision point, using only the information available at that moment?

Here is a replay template we use in coaching:
- Decision point: [timestamp or scenario step]
- Information available at that moment: [what the chart showed, what your rules said]
- What process said to do: [based on your rules, not outcome]
- What you actually did: [your action]
- Gap (if any): [what was different between process and action — and why]

No outcome language in the debrief. Not "I was right" or "I was wrong." What did the process say?

Try this on your last sim run and share what you notice. Use #crypto-journal for the entry.

Market Monday: Position sizing — the one skill that determines whether you survive long enough to get good.

Most beginner traders focus on picking direction. But direction is only half of a trade decision. The other half — how much of your account is at risk on this one position — determines whether a string of losses ends your participation or is just a debrief exercise.

The concept:
- Risk per trade = the percentage of your account you are willing to lose if the trade goes against you.
- Position size = calculated from that risk percentage, not from how "confident" you feel.

A common starting point for process learners: risk no more than 1-2% of your practice account on any single scenario. That means a loss on that trade = 1-2% drawdown, not 20%.

This is not a rule about what *you* should do with real money — it is a concept to understand and practice in sim. Your actual risk tolerance in live markets is your decision, made with a licensed professional if needed.

What is your current approach to sizing in the TradeGame sim? Does it match a defined rule or does it shift based on how the scenario feels? Log it in #stocks-journal.

Replay Friday: Reading the replay without the result.

Here is an exercise for this week's replay session.

Take a scenario replay you have already completed — one where you know the outcome. Before you open the replay, write down:
1. What market condition was this scenario set in? (trending, ranging, choppy?)
2. What was your entry rule?
3. What was your exit rule?
4. Did you follow both?

Now open the replay and check. Not "did I make money" — did I follow my rules? Were the conditions I thought I was trading in the conditions that were actually present?

The gap between what we think we were doing and what the chart shows we were doing is where the real learning is.

## 4. Education-Not-Advice Filter

Run every post through this checklist before publishing. One "yes" on a red-flag question means revise or do not post.

**The core question (COMMUNITY.md §4):**
Does this post teach a concept, show a process, or share a community story? Or does it imply someone should take a specific market action?

If the answer is the second: do not post.

---

**Checklist — run before every public-facing post:**

| Question | Red flag if yes |
|---|---|
| Does the post name a specific asset and suggest it is worth buying, selling, watching, or trading? | Yes = revise |
| Does the post contain price levels with directional language ("watching," "entry," "target," "alert")? | Yes = remove the directional language or do not post |
| Does the post imply a member made money, grew an account, or achieved a financial outcome? | Yes = apply Disclosure Block §A verbatim before posting |
| Does the post use urgency language ("now," "before it moves," "don't miss," "act fast")? | Yes = revise — this is engagement bait and off-brand |
| Does the post promise, imply, or suggest predictable outcomes from using the game, following the community, or working with a coach? | Yes = revise — no performance promises |
| Does the post mention a third-party tool, platform, broker, or service where a material relationship exists, without disclosure? | Yes = apply Disclosure Block §B at the top before posting |
| If the post showcases a cohort graduation or member milestone, does it include the process-only framing and the FTC disclosures? | No = add them before posting |
| Does the §C footer appear on the post (for any public-facing channel or surface)? | No = add it |

**Quick gut check:**
If a regulator or a reporter screenshot this post tomorrow, is it clearly education — or does it look like advice, a signal, or an earnings claim?

If you hesitate: revise or do not post.
