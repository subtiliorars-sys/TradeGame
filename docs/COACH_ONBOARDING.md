# Coach Onboarding — TradeGame

Training pack for new volunteer coaches. Read this in full before signing
COACH_AGREEMENT.md. Signing without reading it is not a shortcut — role
removal for a violation you "didn't know about" still happens.

---

## 1. What process-coaching is

Process-coaching is teaching the craft of trading — how to think, size, plan,
and debrief — without ever telling someone what to trade or when.

The four pillars of every coaching interaction:

**Sizing.** Does this member know how to calculate position size from their
stated stop and risk tolerance? Can they explain why they sized as they did?
That is coachable. "Buy X now, it's a good size here" is not coaching — it's
advice, and it's prohibited.

**Stops.** Does the member have a pre-defined exit rule before they enter a
scenario? Is it based on a price level or a percent-loss discipline? Reviewing
whether a stop was set and followed is coaching. "Put your stop at $42.50" is
advice.

**Plans.** Did the member write out a trade thesis before entering — what
they expected, what would invalidate it, what they would do in each case?
Reading that plan with them and asking questions about its logic is coaching.
Rewriting their plan for them is not.

**Journals and debriefs.** After a scenario: what did the process look like?
Where did it hold? Where did it break? The best coaching moment in TradeGame is
"you followed every rule and still lost — what does that tell you about
risk-reward over a sample?" That is the job.

Scenario replays are the primary teaching tool. The game generates a record of
every decision. Coaches use that record to ask questions, not to hand down
verdicts.

## 2. What process-coaching is NOT — and why the line is hard

You are not permitted to tell any member what to buy, sell, hold, enter, or
exit, for any instrument, in any market, in any channel, call, or DM.

This is not bureaucratic caution. Here is the actual reason:

In the United States (and most jurisdictions with securities markets), giving
personalized investment advice for compensation — or as part of a business that
holds itself out as providing such advice — requires registration as an
Investment Adviser (SEC) or Commodity Trading Adviser (CFTC), or their state
equivalents. The test is not whether money changed hands in that moment. The
test is whether a reasonable person receiving the advice would rely on it for a
real financial decision.

A coach in a trading community who says "I'd buy ETH here" or "that setup looks
good, go for it" — even casually, even with "this isn't advice" appended — is
doing something that looks, to a regulator, like unregistered personalized
investment advice. TradeGame is not registered. The org cannot be registered as
long as it operates as a volunteer community org. Crossing this line puts both
the coach and the org in legal jeopardy, and it destroys the trust that the
community is built on.

The word "education" is not a magic shield. We are safe when we teach
concepts, review process, and let the member own every decision. We are not
safe when we become a decision-maker for them.

## 3. The redirect — three worked examples

The phrase is the same every time: **"That's your decision — let's look at
your process."** Here is how it sounds in practice.

---

**Example A — Beginner asks about a real token in #crypto-learn**

> Member: "Should I buy SOL right now? It's been dipping a lot and seems like
> a good entry."

Redirect:
> "I can't call entries — that's outside what I do here, and it's outside what
> anyone in this community should do. What I can help with: have you written out
> what your risk tolerance is for a position like this? What would your stop
> look like? If you want to share your sizing math or a journal entry on your
> thinking, let's dig into the process."

What you did: refused the entry call, explained briefly why, offered a
concrete alternative. You did not say "I don't know" or leave them without a
path forward.

---

**Example B — Cohort member asks mid-replay**

> Member (in cohort session replay review): "Looking back at this, I really
> should have held longer, right? The chart was showing strength."

Redirect:
> "Let's separate what the chart showed from what your plan said. When you
> opened this scenario, what was your stated exit rule? Did you follow it? —
> That's the question. Whether the chart 'showed strength' after your exit is
> outcome thinking. We're here to evaluate the decision against your stated
> rules, not against what the price did afterward."

What you did: blocked the outcome-reasoning trap, brought focus back to the
plan they wrote before the trade. You did not validate or challenge the market
observation — you made it irrelevant to the debrief.

---

**Example C — Someone asks for advice in a voice session (live)**

> Voice participant: "Quick question — do you think XRP is a good hold right
> now given everything happening with the SEC?"

Redirect (on-air, immediately):
> "I'm going to stop you there — that's a call on a live asset, and I can't
> make those, not even as an opinion. If you want to talk through how you'd
> analyze a regulatory event's effect on your own risk framework, or how to
> journal around a high-uncertainty position, bring that to me. But the
> direction question is yours."

What you did: cut it cleanly, did not hedge or leave it ambiguous, redirected
to something coachable. The redirect happened on-air and is in the recording.
That matters — see the voice-surface rules below.

## 4. Replay-review session format

Every replay review follows this structure. There is no improvised format.

**Before the session**

- Confirm you have admin access to the replay (coach-selected, or member's
  with documented consent).
- Post the session thread in #replay-review 24 hours before with: scenario
  type, whose replay (or "anonymous community replay"), and the one or two
  process questions you plan to focus on.
- Confirm recording is set up OR a second staff member is present (see §5).

**During the session**

1. Open with the disclaimer, stated aloud: "This is an educational review of
   game-sim decisions. Nothing said here is financial advice or a
   recommendation to take any action in real markets."
2. Walk through the replay chronologically. At each significant decision point,
   pause and ask: "What information was available here? What rule applies? Was
   the rule followed?"
3. Keep discussion inside the sim. If anyone references what a real market did
   on the same day, redirect: "We're staying in the sim — the point is the
   process, not the outcome."
4. Name the pattern you are observing: "Sized too large relative to the stated
   stop — that's the pattern here, not bad luck."

**The good-process/bad-outcome celebration pattern**

This is one of the most important things you do. When a member followed every
rule and still lost the scenario:

> "This is the best kind of result to review. Good process, bad outcome.
> The process is what you control. Over a sample of twenty trades with this
> kind of sizing discipline, what do you expect? That is the question —
> not 'why did this one lose?'"

Do not let the session end without naming this explicitly when it applies.
Members who internalize "good process is a win" become better traders. Members
who learn to chase outcomes become the community risk.

**After the session**

- Post a written summary in the thread: decisions reviewed, process patterns
  identified, one take-home per pattern. No outcome scorekeeping. No "here's
  what I would have done."
- Log the session in the #cohort-announcements or your cohort thread as
  completed.

## 5. Voice-surface rules

Every cohort call, office hours session, and AMA you run:

- **Recorded OR two-staff-present.** There are no exceptions. If neither
  condition can be met before a session starts, postpone the session. An
  unrecorded, solo-staffed voice session cannot be spot-checked and should
  not happen. (Source: COACH_AGREEMENT §5; RISK_REGISTER §24.)
- **Questions pre-screened for AMAs.** For scheduled AMA formats, collect
  questions in text first. Filter out any that ask for asset direction or
  specific advice. You can acknowledge "we got a lot of questions about
  whether to buy X — I won't answer those, but here's what I can discuss
  about the risk framework around that kind of decision."
- **When someone asks for advice live.** See Example C above. Redirect on-air,
  clearly, immediately. Do not pause, go off-mic, or soften it. The redirect
  needs to be in the recording because the recording is the spot-check record.
- **If the recording fails mid-session** and you are solo-staffed: announce
  it in the session, wrap up within five minutes, and reschedule the remainder.
  Log what happened.

## 6. Spot-check expectations

Your sessions will be sampled. The Founder or a designated monitor reviews a
sample of each cohort's sessions within one week of delivery, using the
COHORT_SPOTCHECK_CHECKLIST.md criteria.

This is not surveillance — it is quality culture. The spot-check exists because
the org carries compliance exposure if coaching drifts into advice, and because
members deserve consistent standards regardless of which coach they land with.

What a spot-check looks at (from the checklist):
- Recording or two-staff notes exist (if neither: that is itself a finding).
- No buy/sell/hold/enter/exit guidance, named or unnamed instrument.
- No market-timing direction (timing language without a ticker still fails).
- "Should I trade X?" moments redirected, not answered.
- No coach PnL bragging; no member-win showcasing without proper disclosure.
- No tool/bot/token promotion beyond mechanics description.
- No payment solicitation or acceptance.
- Disclaimer stated or visible at session start.

A PASS is logged and that's it. A FINDING gets the relevant rule quoted to
you — treat it as a calibration, not a punishment. A repeat finding on the
same rule, or any payment-related finding, triggers immediate role removal per
COACH_AGREEMENT §7. No appeal.

## 7. No-DM-first and no-personal-monetization rules

**No DM first.** You never DM a member first about trading, coaching, or
anything related to the coaching role. If a member contacts you, you can
respond in-platform. But you do not initiate. This rule exists because the
most common impersonation scam pattern is someone posing as a coach and sliding
into DMs to extract money or pump assets. By making "coaches never DM first"
a hard community rule, we protect members. Breaking this rule — even once, even
with good intent — corrodes that protection for everyone.

If you see someone impersonating a coach in DMs, direct the report to
#scam-reports immediately.

**No personal monetization.** You accept no payment, tip, donation,
subscription, token, or gift from any member for coaching. No Venmo, CashApp,
Patreon, crypto wallet, no exceptions. If a member offers to pay you: decline
clearly, do not redirect them to a personal payment method "outside the org."

Why this matters beyond your own role: if a coach accepts compensation for
coaching in this context, it makes both the coach and the org look like an
unregistered investment adviser operating commercially. It is the fastest path
to the kind of regulatory and reputational harm that ends communities. Any
violation ends your coach role immediately, with no grace period.

## 8. Graduation post rules (FTC)

When a cohort completes and you post or approve a graduation post in
#cohort-announcements:

- **Process achievements only.** Post how the cohort showed up — journaling
  consistency, scenario completion, debrief quality. Not outcomes.
- **No outcome showcasing.** Do not mention, imply, or display any member's
  sim PnL, "winning trades," or results in a way that could suggest they became
  profitable. Simulated results are not real trading results, and presenting
  them as achievements is an FTC earnings-claim risk.
- **Disclosure block required if any benefit exists.** If any graduating member
  received any benefit from the org — early game access, cosmetics, a coaching
  role — that relationship must be disclosed in the post. Use the template from
  docs/templates/DISCLOSURE_BLOCK.md. When in doubt, disclose.
- **"Results not typical" applies always.** Even for process achievements
  ("she completed 30 journal entries"), do not frame them as something the next
  cohort can expect to replicate. Every member's path is their own.
